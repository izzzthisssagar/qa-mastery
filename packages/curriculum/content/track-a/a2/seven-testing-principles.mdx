## Seven rules that decide how you spend your time

The seven testing principles are not trivia for an exam. They are the reasons a smart tester does not try to test everything, does not trust a wall of green checkmarks, and does not run the same script forever. Watch them play out on BuggyShop, our practice store, and they stop being slogans.

## See it — the seven, made concrete

| # | Principle | On BuggyShop |
|---|-----------|--------------|
| 1 | Testing shows presence, not absence, of defects | 200 passing tests do not prove checkout is correct; they prove the 200 cases you checked passed |
| 2 | Exhaustive testing is impossible | The coupon field accepts any string. You cannot try every string in a lifetime |
| 3 | Early testing (shift-left) | A misread spec for tax caught in review costs minutes; caught after release costs a refund storm |
| 4 | Defects cluster | Most bugs pile up in signup and checkout, not the static "About" page |
| 5 | Pesticide paradox | Your v1.0 regression suite keeps passing while the v1.1 bug walks straight past it |
| 6 | Testing is context-dependent | You test a payment flow harder than a footer link; a banking app harder than a blog |
| 7 | Absence-of-errors fallacy | Checkout has zero bugs but charges in the wrong currency — defect-free, still useless |

## Try it — spot the pesticide paradox

BuggyShop ships v1.1. You rerun the exact v1.0 regression suite: all 60 tests green. A user then reports that applying two coupons stacks discounts below zero and pays the customer. Your suite never tried two coupons, because in v1.0 stacking was impossible.

Which principle just bit you? The tests did not get worse; they got stale. They keep checking v1.0 behaviour and never probe the new v1.1 path. That is the pesticide paradox (#5): reused tests stop finding new bugs. The fix is to add cases for the changed feature, not to rerun the old ones harder. Notice #1 hiding underneath: all-green proved presence-of-nothing-found, not absence of defects.

## Do it — find the cluster on BuggyShop

Open BuggyShop's bug log (or your own notes from earlier lessons). Tally how many defects fall in each area: signup, login, product page, cart, checkout, footer. You will almost always see signup and checkout dominate — that is #4, defects cluster. Now write one sentence justifying where you would spend the next testing hour, citing #4 and #6 (context: checkout touches money).
