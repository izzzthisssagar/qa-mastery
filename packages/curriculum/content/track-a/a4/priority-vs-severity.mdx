## Two questions, not one

When a bug arrives, two different people are asking two different questions. The engineer asks "how badly does this break?" The product owner asks "do we fix it before we ship?" Confuse the two and you ship the wrong thing - or block a release over a typo. Triage is the discipline of answering both, on purpose.

## See it - they are different axes

Severity = technical impact. Priority = business urgency. They are independent, so every bug lands somewhere on a grid:

| Bug | Severity | Priority | Why |
|-----|----------|----------|-----|
| Checkout throws 500, no orders go through | High | High | Breaks the core flow, costs money every minute |
| Logo typo on homepage, demo tomorrow | Low | High | Trivial technically, but the CEO sees it on stage |
| Admin yearly CSV export crashes | High | Low | Total failure, but one user, used once a year |
| Footer copyright year says 2023 | Low | Low | Cosmetic, nobody is blocked |

The two "diagonal" cases are the whole point: low severity can still be high priority, and high severity can still wait.

## Try it - place the bug

A bug report comes in: "On the mobile app, tapping 'Share' on a product copies a link with a trailing space, so pasted links 404."

Reason it through. Technical impact: the link is broken - that is functionally significant, not cosmetic, so severity is medium-to-high. Business urgency: sharing drives new-customer traffic and a marketing campaign launches Friday. So priority is high. This bug sits in the high/high-ish corner and should jump the queue. Notice you reached two answers from two questions, not one gut feeling.

## Do it - triage a BuggyShop batch

Open BuggyShop and find or invent five defects: one true blocker, one cosmetic-but-embarrassing item near a "launch", one catastrophic failure in an obscure corner, one minor annoyance, and one of your choice. For each, write a one-line severity rating, a one-line priority rating, and a single sentence of reasoning that names the affected users and the technical impact separately. If your reasoning for severity and priority is identical, you have not actually separated the axes - rewrite it.
