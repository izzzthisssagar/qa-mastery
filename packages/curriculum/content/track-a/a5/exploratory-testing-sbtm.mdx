## Why "just clicking around" is a skill, not a shrug

When a tester says "I explored the app," people imagine random clicking. Done well, exploratory testing is the opposite of random. You learn the product, design a test, and run it — all at once — steering each move with what the last one taught you. It finds the bugs scripts miss because scripts can only catch what someone already imagined.

The risk is that "freedom to explore" turns into untracked, unrepeatable work. Session-Based Test Management (SBTM) is the structure that keeps it accountable: a charter to aim you, a time-box to focus you, and notes to prove what you did.

## See it — scripted vs exploratory

| Aspect        | Scripted testing                  | Exploratory testing                     |
|---------------|-----------------------------------|-----------------------------------------|
| Test design   | Written in advance                | Done live, during execution             |
| Guidance      | Step-by-step expected results     | A charter (mission) + tester judgment   |
| Best at       | Regression, known requirements    | New features, vague areas, "what if?"   |
| Output        | Pass/fail per step                | Session notes, bugs, new questions      |

Neither wins outright. Scripted testing locks down known behavior; exploratory testing probes the unknown. Mature teams run both.

## Try it — read a charter

A charter is one or two sentences. Picture this one:

> Explore the checkout flow with expired and near-expiry coupon codes to discover pricing and validation issues. (60 min)

Notice what it gives you and what it withholds. It gives a target (checkout coupons), a risk to hunt (pricing/validation), and a time-box (60 min). It does *not* give steps — you decide whether to try a code that expired yesterday, one expiring mid-session, or a valid code applied twice. The charter focuses you; your judgment fills in the moves. As you go, you jot notes: what you tried, what happened, and any new question worth a future session.

## Do it — write your own charter

Open BuggyShop and log in as a test user with some order history. Your mission: probe **profile and order history for data-integrity issues** in a 30-minute session.

Write a charter before you touch anything. Use this shape:

> Explore [area] using [data/conditions] to discover [type of issue]. (30 min)

Fill it in for this target. Then list 4 to 5 concrete things you'd actually try — for example: does the order total on the history page match the sum of line items? Does editing your profile email change the name shown on past orders? Do cancelled orders still count toward "total spent"? Keep timestamped notes as you go, and end with a one-line debrief: what you covered, what you found, what you'd explore next.
