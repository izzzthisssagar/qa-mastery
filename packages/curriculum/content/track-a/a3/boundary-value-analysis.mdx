## Where do bugs live?

Not in the middle. A field that accepts **1–99** almost never breaks at 50. It
breaks at **0**. At **1**. At **99**. At **100**.

**Bugs live at the edges.** This lesson teaches you to hunt there — finding more
defects with **6 well-chosen tests** than a colleague throwing 99 random ones.

Notice what happened at the edges? A developer who writes `if (qty > 0)` when
they meant `qty >= 1`, or `<` when they meant `<=`, creates a defect that is
**invisible everywhere except the boundary**. Off-by-one errors are the single
most common logic mistake in software.

### The two techniques

- **2-value BVA** — for each boundary, test the boundary itself and its nearest
  invalid neighbour. Range 1–99 → test **{0, 1, 99, 100}**.
- **3-value BVA** — the stricter variant adds the nearest *valid* neighbour
  inside the range: **{0, 1, 2, 98, 99, 100}**. Reach for it when the cost of an
  escaped defect is high — payments, age limits, legal thresholds.

> BVA needs partitions first. Boundaries only exist where a valid partition meets
> an invalid one — which is why Equivalence Partitioning (A3.2) comes right before
> this lesson.

## Try it — your turn

A discount-code field accepts codes **5–10 characters** long. Before you read on,
work out the 2-value BVA set in your head.

- How many boundaries does a range have? **Two** — a lower and an upper.
- What's the invalid neighbour just below 5? **4.** Just above 10? **11.**
- So the 2-value set is **{4, 5, 10, 11}** — two values per boundary.

That's the whole discipline: find the partitions, then test each edge and the
value just outside it.
