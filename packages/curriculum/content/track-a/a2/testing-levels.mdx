## Test at the right altitude

The same product is tested at several **levels**, each catching different defects:

| Level | Scope | Usually owned by | Catches |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Unit** | One function/component in isolation | Developers | Logic errors in a small piece |
| **Integration** | Two+ pieces working together | Devs / QA | Broken seams, bad contracts |
| **System** | The whole assembled product | QA | End-to-end behaviour vs requirements |
| **Acceptance (UAT)** | The product vs business need | Users / business | "Is this actually what we wanted?" |

## See it — where a bug gets caught

A defect in a price-rounding function:

- **Unit** test on that function → caught immediately, cheapest.
- Missed at unit? **Integration** might catch it when the cart sums lines.
- Missed there? **System** test of checkout might see a wrong total.
- Missed there too? **UAT** — or a customer — finds it. Most expensive.

Lower levels are cheaper and more precise; higher levels are broader but costlier.

## Try it — who owns what

A common confusion: "testing" only means the QA-run system level. In reality unit
and much integration testing is **developer**-owned; system testing is the manual
tester's home turf; UAT is the **business/user's** call. Knowing the owner tells
you where to push when a class of defect keeps slipping through.

4. Users say the whole returns flow doesn't match how the business actually works.
